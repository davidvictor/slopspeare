# How conflicts were resolved

Slopspeare aggregates rules from several sources that did not always agree. Where two sources pulled in different directions, the guide picked one position and recorded the reasoning here, so a contributor can see why a rule reads the way it does and reopen the question with evidence.

The general principle: when sources conflict, the guide favors the reading that removes the machine tell, even when that means overriding a stylistic preference that predates the current wave of language models.

## 1. The em dash

**The disagreement.** One source treats the em dash as a signature punctuation mark for asides, the most expressive tool in a writer's kit. Most other sources, and the strictest voice among them, treat the in-sentence em dash as the single clearest tell of machine-generated text.

**The resolution.** The guide bans the in-sentence em dash outright (`PUNCT-1`). The mark is now so strongly associated with generated text that the cost of using it outweighs the expressive gain, and a period, comma, or colon covers almost every case. A writer who wants the aside-dash as a conscious style choice can opt back in knowing it reads as a tell to many readers. The dash between two numbers stays allowed, because that is typographic range work rather than sentence punctuation.

## 2. Short sentences versus stacked fragments

**The disagreement.** One source champions short declarative sentences as the backbone of strong copy. Another bans stacked fragments and staccato cadence as performative.

**The resolution.** Both are kept and reconciled as one principle (`STRUCT-2`, `STRUCT-3`): vary rhythm on purpose. Short sentences are welcome for impact and clarity. A wall of clipped fragments is the failure mode. The dividing line is whether the short sentences sit inside varied cadence or replace it.

## 3. Contrast and "what not to do" framing

**The disagreement.** Some copywriting sources endorse anti-pattern framing ("here is what most people get wrong") as a way to show judgment. Other sources ban the reflexive "X, not Y" contrast as a top tell.

**The resolution.** The "X, not Y" reformulation is a hard ban (`RHET-1`). Genuine contrast is allowed only when it is load-bearing and carries a specific reason, written as a separate sentence rather than the comma-not shape. Reflexive contrast reached for as rhetorical punch is never allowed.

## 4. Reflection and lessons learned

**The disagreement.** Several copy sources encourage closing with honest reflection and a named failure. The strictest voice bans fabricated self-criticism and invented hindsight.

**The resolution.** Reflection is allowed only when it is genuine, specific, and supplied or confirmed by the author (`TONE-10`). Inventing a flaw or a "what I'd do differently" to fill a slot is banned (`SUBST-10`). When the reflection is real, keep the tone observational rather than confessional.

## 5. Formal transition words

**The disagreement.** Academic and document-generation registers tolerate connectors like "furthermore" and "moreover." Other sources cut or cap them as machine register.

**The resolution.** "Furthermore," "moreover," and "additionally" are treated as high-signal machine vocabulary and cut (`VOCAB-6`). Formal transitions are capped at roughly two per piece, and often the cleanest connector is none.

## What was generalized out

Several source rules were specific to one person's portfolio or one tool's workflow. Slopspeare keeps the transferable kernel and drops the local specifics. A few examples:

- **Exact metric counts** ("use 3 or 6, never 4") became the general idea that you should not pad to a round or even count to look complete.
- **A specific banned catchphrase** ("every layer in one head") became the general rule to find and cut your own recurring crutch phrases (`VOCAB-10`).
- **Portfolio heading conventions** (a hard word limit, one italicized word) became the general rules to vary heading shape and make a heading a claim (`FORMAT-6`).
- **A tool's exact 18-word ban list and curated jargon file** became the tiered vocabulary rules and the gloss-jargon rule (`VOCAB-1`, `VOCAB-2`, `SUBST-8`).
- **Channel-specific length rules** (a 20-word average for one social platform) became the rule to match density to how the text will be read (`FORMAT-7`).

If you maintain a house style, the right pattern is to adopt Slopspeare as the base and layer your specifics on top, rather than fork the whole thing.
